# Masterclass: Defeating Defense (Yuki Lee Bender)

## Value vs Deck Damage

Two metrics that decide who wins in FaB:

**Value**: How efficient each turn is in terms of total damage blocked + total damage threatened. The player who consistently finds higher value over the course of the game should win. Example: if you attack 8 / block 6 (14 value) vs opponent's attack 7 / block 6 (13 value), you gain 1 life advantage per turn cycle.

**Deck Damage**: If one player runs out of cards before life totals hit 0, that player cannot win. Winning through deck damage means pulling ahead in cards-in-deck.

Ways to pull ahead on deck damage:
- **Weapon swings**: Pitch card returns to bottom of deck; opponent loses a card blocking. Net +1 card advantage.
- **Big attacks**: e.g. Cadaverous Tilling for 8 — trades 1 card for 2 blocks + 2 life. Much better deck damage than small attacks like Head Jab (1-for-1).

**Assessing deck damage position**: Count total cards in hand + pitch zone + arsenal for each player. Add (life difference ÷ 3) to the player with higher life. Example: you have 30 cards + 15 life lead = effectively "35" cards vs opponent's 32.

**Critical insight**: The game is decided by only ONE of these factors. A life lead doesn't matter if you fatigue first. A deck lead doesn't matter if your life hits 0. Understanding which axis matters is key.

## Fatigue

Game state where a player runs out of cards and becomes functionally unable to win. Weapons that threaten damage without spending deck cards dominate this state.

Not all defensive decks rely on fatigue — some threaten alternate win conditions. Understanding how important fatigue is to a specific deck and how it plans to get there is key.

## Three Types of Defensive Strategies

### 1. Attrition

**Examples**: Life gain Oldhim, Count Your Blessings Enigma/Jarl

**Gameplan**: Win through deck damage and fatigue. Good defense + big attacks + strong weapon. Keep playing defense until opponent runs out of threats, then weapon takes over.

**Still need to respect value**: If opponents stumble, attrition decks may heal, threaten damage, or play disruption like Command and Conquer.

**How to beat attrition**:
- **Create very high damage turns** that exceed their ability to defend (e.g. Aurora with Arc Lightning, Viserai Runechant stacking)
- **Strong recursive weapon value** — repeatedly threaten lots of weapon damage while also playing offensively to out-deck-damage them (e.g. Fai with Searing Emberblade + Phoenix Flames endgame loop: ability activation → Phoenix Flame → Searing Emberblade → ability → Phoenix Flame = 5 damage without deck cards)

### 2. Disruption

**Examples**: Nuu, defensive Bravo Showstopper with Spinal Crush/Crippling Crush

**Gameplan**: Block with 1-3 cards, then threaten disruptive attacks. Lower opponent's output by forcing them to block or suffer debilitating on-hits (Persuasive Prognosis, Bonds of Agony, Command and Conquer). Straddle the line between value and deck damage.

**The trap**: If you only block disruption → can't get their life to 0 before they fatigue you. If you eat too much damage trying to set up → life gets so low everything becomes a must-block → easy fatigue.

**How to beat disruption**:
- **Defense reactions** — great for evading disruption, especially vs Nuu's attack reactions
- **Equipment timing is critical** — only the way to block without reducing hand size. Only use equipment when you can also threaten big damage in return. Don't squander equipment for mediocre turns.
- **Avoid medium-size hands** — their bread and butter is 2-3 card hands. If you keep returning 2-3 card hands you play into their gameplan.
- **Force damage-or-disruption decisions**: Disruptive attacks are usually 2-3 card plays. Without arsenal they block ~6-7 to play disruption → present >7 damage to force a choice. With arsenal they block ~9-10 → present >10 to force their life lower.
- **Trade small hands until they miss disruption**: If your return will be small, do extra blocking, establish arsenal, force them to disrupt again. They may miss drawing disruption → you play a 5-card hand which can be game-winning.
- **Arsenal cards that play around arsenal disruption** (e.g. Aurora's Channel Lightning Valley, Electromagnetic Somersault)

**Key insight**: Disruption decks are the most challenging to play against because they threaten on both axes. Leaning too far in either direction is punishing. Takes practice to read which focus matters at each game stage.

### 3. Set Up

**Examples**: Runechant-stacking Florian, Pistol Dash

**Gameplan**: Play excellent defense while building to a very strong endgame. Progress gameplan without attacking (Runechants, Induction Chambers, Plasma Purifiers). Ask opponents if they can push through defenses before the inevitable endgame arrives.

**Strength**: Can progress without attacking → opponents can't block to trade value. Strong counter to attrition and disruption decks.

**How to beat set up**:
- **Go faster** — maximize value early, especially offensive pressure. These decks have endgame inevitability, so speed is the answer.
- **5-card hand / 3-card hand rhythm**: Play on-turn with 5-card hands (no arsenal), then 3-card hands ending on arsenal to set up another 5-card hand. Pushes life total quickly.
- **Cindra and Fai** are heroes that punish set up strategies (why Cindra resurged in Pro Quest Singapore)
- **Offensive packages** in otherwise defensive decks: dagger pumps for Arakni, Pummel/Zealous Belting/Rouse the Ancients for Guardian

## Additional Counters to Defensive Decks

- **Combo heroes** (Kano, Verdance): Given enough time, set up extremely large turns that exceed what defensive decks can reasonably block
- **Dual-wielding Warriors**: Good defense + value + recursive weapon damage → line up well vs attrition and disruption, but may struggle to pressure set up decks
- **Illusionists** (board-state builders): Defensive decks struggle to pressure and manage board state. Enigma was the prime example; Gravy Bones may fill this role.
