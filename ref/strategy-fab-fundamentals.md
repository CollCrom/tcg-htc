# Strategic Guide to Flesh and Blood (Ultimate Guard)
Source: https://ultimateguard.com/en/blog/strategic-guide-flesh-and-blood-beginners-intermediates-fab

## Core Value System

**The Foundation**: "One card is worth 3 points." Each card should defend for 3, attack for 3, or generate 3 resources when pitched.

**Point Definition**: A point equals one life point or one resource. All value directly affects life totals.

**Card Color Values**:
- Red cards: 3 attack or 3 defense, on-rate by themselves
- Blue cards: 2 attack/defense but generate 3 resources when pitched
- Yellow cards: 2 attack/defense but generate 2 resources, offer flexibility

**Action Point Bonus**: Attack action cards gain +1 value for consuming your action (no Go Again). This bonus helps cards reach the 3-point rate threshold.

## Hand Evaluation Process

1. Calculate offensive value (all cards attacking)
2. Calculate defensive value (all cards blocking)
3. Consider mixed lines (some blocking, some attacking)
4. Account for arsenal placement value
5. Determine which line maximizes total value

## Arsenal Strategy

**High-Value Arsenal Uses**:
- Combo pieces waiting for their synergy partner
- Starter cards (Go Again effects) for hands needing them
- Good red cards as backup for all-blue hands
- Defense reactions for crucial opponent turns

**Arsenal Liabilities**:
- Blue cards lose 1 value (can't pitch from arsenal)
- Cards with no cost brick the arsenal
- Random draw-offs may be worth 0 if unplayable

## Breakpoints

Attack values that create awkward defensive decisions: 1, 4, 7 damage leak through; 2 and 5 leak less efficiently.

**Why They Matter**:
- Opponents can't efficiently double-block awkward numbers
- Creates forced inefficient defensive patterns
- Becomes critical when opponent reaches 1 life

**Enhanced Breakpoints**: Pairing breakpoint attacks with on-hit effects increases practical value.

**Defensive Equipment**: Reserve equipment block value for preventing on-hit triggers, not just maintaining life totals.

## Implied Value

Hidden value gained by forcing opponent defensive inefficiency or denying optimal plays.

- Evasion effects (Overpower) denying multi-card blocks
- Small attacks forcing clunky defensive patterns
- Disruptive effects denying opponent's setup turns

## Tempo and Full Play

**Full Tempo**: Keep all cards for offense while opponent depletes cards through forced blocking.

**Breaking Cycles**:
- Patient, defensive play while maintaining high life
- Strategic damage when opponent bricks
- Classes excelling at disruption (Assassin, Ranger, Guardian)

**Off-Turn Setups**: Sacrifice current turn's damage to prepare massive next turn (arsenal, items, auras).

## Fatigue and Deck Value

Games reach 2nd or 3rd cycle through deck. Only 3 cards drawn when deck depleted.

**Key Principles**:
- Cards you pitch eventually recycle — blocking inefficiently with strong cards preserves deck potential
- Weapon swings = free card advantage (no deck cards pitched)
- Attacking generates better fatigue advantage than blocking
- 2-blocks particularly dangerous in fatigue games (wastes card advantage)
- Never play fatigue value exclusively — blocking every turn loses tempo

## Equal Value Decisions

When numbers match, choose based on game state:
- **Fatigue**: blocking preserves more deck value
- **Tempo**: taking damage accelerates game, increases variance
- **Breakpoints**: evaluate which line creates worse blocking math for opponent
- **Arsenal**: consider whether defensive line sets up better future pieces
- **General heuristic**: "This game tends to reward patience" — defensive lines often superior when equal

## Core Strategic Principles

1. Every hand is a puzzle — stop and think before blocking
2. Synergy multiplies value — combos often exceed rate
3. Precision matters — games decided at 1 life
4. Flexibility valuable — yellow cards justify lower raw stats via resources
5. Future planning — arsenal and upcoming turns influence current blocking
6. Life total management — staying high enables full hands
7. Patience rewarded — defensive plays often outvalue aggressive ones when equal
